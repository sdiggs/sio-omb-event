The Alaska Municipal League (AML) appreciates the opportunity to comment on the Office of Management and Budget’s proposed revisions to federal financial assistance regulations. AML represents Alaska’s cities and boroughs and works to strengthen local governments’ ability to provide essential public services, maintain critical infrastructure, and meet state and federal requirements. We urge OMB to withdraw the proposal or substantially revise it after meaningful consultation with local governments, Tribal governments, state agencies, and federal grant recipients. Federal financial assistance is central to municipal government in Alaska. Communities rely on federal awards to maintain roads, ports, water and wastewater systems, public safety, emergency response, housing, energy resilience, broadband, planning capacity, and disaster recovery. These programs must be governed by rules that are clear, predictable, fair, and administrable. The proposed rule would increase uncertainty, expand federal agency discretion, add administrative burden, and create new financial risks for local governments. These concerns are especially acute in Alaska, where municipalities operate in a high-cost environment, often with limited staff, constrained revenue bases, short construction seasons, supply-chain challenges, and communities accessible only by air or water. Municipalities make long-term commitments based on federal awards, including hiring staff, entering contracts, providing matching funds, completing environmental review, coordinating with engineers, procuring materials, and scheduling work around seasonal constraints. If awards can be delayed, suspended, or terminated based on shifting federal priorities, local governments cannot responsibly plan or deliver projects. In Alaska, missing a construction season can add substantial cost and defer critical public health, safety, and infrastructure improvements for a year or more. AML also strongly opposes expanded termination or suspension authority that would allow agencies to end awards because they no longer align with agency priorities or broad national interest standards. Federal awards to local governments are intergovernmental commitments, not discretionary contracts that can be reversed when priorities change. Mid-project termination could leave municipalities with stranded costs, unfinished infrastructure, contractor claims, and service disruptions. Termination authority should be limited to material noncompliance, lack of statutory authority, or circumstances clearly identified in the award at the time of issuance, with notice, appeal rights, transition protections, and reimbursement of allowable costs incurred in good faith. We are also concerned that new policy requirements could be ambiguous, difficult to administer, or unrelated to the statutory purposes of individual grant programs. Local governments need clear compliance obligations. Vague or subjective standards create risk for recipients and subrecipients and may be interpreted inconsistently across agencies. Federal grant conditions should be tied to authorizing statutes, program purposes, and objective compliance requirements. The proposed rule is likely to increase burdens on pass-through entities and subrecipients, which municipalities may serve as, depending on the program; additional reporting, monitoring and compliance duties will require staff capacity that many small local governments do not have. Municipalities cannot absorb new federal duties without additional administrative funding, technical assistance, safe harbors, and proportional requirements based on award size and recipient capacity. AML supports responsible stewardship of federal funds, including appropriate fraud prevention and payment verification. However, new pre-payment screening, Do Not Pay, E-Verify, or related requirements must not delay reimbursements. Reimbursement delays create serious cash-flow challenges for small governments managing capital projects with high upfront costs. OMB should ensure prompt payment timelines for local governments when delays or data inaccuracies are caused by federal systems. AML respectfully urges OMB to withdraw the proposed rule. If OMB proceeds, it should substantially revise the proposal to preserve merit-based and program-specific award decisions; limit termination authority; reduce pass-through and subrecipient burden; protect timely reimbursement; and allow a meaningful transition period. Alaska municipalities are committed to transparency, accountability, and responsible stewardship of federal funds. Those goals are best achieved through clear, stable, and workable rules that respect the intergovernmental partnership between federal agencies and local governments. As written, the proposed rule would increase uncertainty, administrative burden, and financial risk while making it harder for communities to deliver essential services and infrastructure.

American Ceramic Society
2026-06-30 · OMB-2026-0034-43684
To the Office of Management and Budget: The American Ceramic Society (ACerS) submits this comment to oppose the proposed treatment of publication costs under § 200.461. ACerS is a nonprofit scientific society founded in 1898 and based in Westerville, Ohio. We publish four peer-reviewed journals in partnership with Wiley, serving the global ceramics and glass research community across academia, national laboratories, and industry. Revenue from our publishing program is reinvested directly into peer review, editorial operations, and programs that bring students and early career researchers into the field. The proposed rule would generally make publication costs, including article publication/processing charges (APCs) and open access (OA) fees, unallowable under federal awards unless required by statute or approved by an agency on a case-by-case basis. We urge OMB to strike or substantially revise this provision. As written, it would reduce the dissemination of federally funded ceramics and glass research, harm nonprofit society publishers, and conflict with the federal government's own open access commitments. The impact on ACerS is concrete and measurable: One of our four journals, the International Journal of Ceramic Engineering & Science, is fully open access, meaning every article it publishes is supported by an APC rather than subscriptions. Researchers funded by federal agencies who wish to publish their results in that journal would no longer have a clear, allowable mechanism to do so. The case-by-case approval pathway in the proposed rule does not provide the predictability authors need when planning where to publish and would cause substantial delays to the publishing process. This could ultimately harm graduate students who are trying to publish their work within a short, often 4-to-5-year window to graduate. Across all four of our journals in 2025, 37% of the OA articles we published were funded by a federal grant that paid the APC. For two of our journals in particular, the Journal of the American Ceramic Society (JACerS), our flagship journal, and the International Journal of Applied Glass Science (IJAGS), over 40% of the published OA articles in 2025 were paid for by a federal grant. Removing that funding pathway would not simply shift the cost elsewhere. It would suppress open access publishing of federally funded ceramics and glass research, push that research behind subscription access where it reaches fewer readers, or discourage publication of the work entirely. This would especially impact smaller institutions who cannot afford to pay for a subscription. The provision also works against existing federal policy. Federal agencies have spent years moving toward immediate public access to taxpayer-funded research. Making publication and open access costs unallowable by default directly conflicts with those open access requirements. The result would be an unfunded mandate: researchers would still be expected to make their work publicly accessible but would lose the established means of paying for it. Finally, the rationale that publication costs are discretionary or serve reputational rather than programmatic interests misunderstands how scientific research works. Publication is not an optional add-on to a federal award. It is how results are validated through peer review, corrected, archived, and made usable by the next researcher. We respectfully request that OMB remove the restriction on publication costs in § 200.461 and preserve APCs and open access fees as allowable costs under federal awards.

The American Partnership for Eosinophilic Disorders (APFED) submits this comment on behalf of its Executive Board, Executive Director, and the community it serves, in strong opposition to proposed revisions of 2 CFR. APFED is a nonprofit patient advocacy organization dedicated to improving the lives of those affected by eosinophilic diseases — rare, chronic, poorly understood diseases with limited treatment options. Patients rely on scientifically rigorous, merit-driven federal research. For eosinophilic diseases, the path from basic science to approved therapy requires sustained federal investment. APFED works alongside NIH-funded researchers, physician-scientists, and academic medical centers to provide patient impact and research priorities feedback. This partnership depends on confidence that funded research will be conducted and completed as designed. The proposal to terminate any active federal award at any point because a political appointee determines inconsistency with administration priorities (§200.340) destroys confidence. Rare disease research involves: • Multi-year cohort studies tracking progression and treatment response • Mechanistic studies requiring longitudinal follow-up • Outcomes research that builds on sequential data collection • Natural history studies None can be interrupted and resumed. Termination for political reasons — without scientific failure or noncompliance — abandons patients, invalidates accumulated data, and sets progress back years. Rare diseases are apolitical. The peer review system protects rare disease research by ensuring scientific merit is the criterion for federal funding. This rule eliminates that protection. Under §200.205 and §200.205(d), political appointees are required to approve every discretionary grant and cannot defer to peer reviewers. Reviewers' recommendations become non-binding. These appointees are not required to have scientific qualifications, and the criteria they apply — including whether a grant "demonstrably advances the President's policy priorities" — have no relationship to scientific merit or patient need. APFED is deeply concerned that rare disease research will be systematically disadvantaged under a system of political gatekeeper review. The research our patients depend on is unlikely to be a priority for political appointees whose evaluative criteria are defined in terms of current administration policy, not patient outcomes. The poorly defined "Gold Standard Science" requirement (§200.205) functions as unchecked discretion to favor or disfavor research programs on political grounds. Institutional prestige and scientific track record are explicitly deprioritized. For emerging rare disease research centers, this is a direct threat. Statistically meaningful research on rare diseases requires international collaboration, data sharing across patient registries, and clinical networks. APFED supports these partnerships as no single country has a patient population large enough to power research the field requires. The proposed prohibition on collaboration with entities in broadly designated foreign countries (§200.220), combined with the domestic-first framework requiring written approval from a senior political appointee for any international research collaboration (§200.202(e)), effectively ends international partnerships on which rare disease research depends. This is not a targeted national security measure; it is a presumption against international science that most harms fields dependent on global collaboration. APFED's work involves translating scientific research into information that patients and families can use, and channeling patient experience back into research priority-setting. This requires open, ongoing communication between researchers, advocacy organizations, and patient communities. Under this proposed rule: • The issue advocacy prohibition (§200.450) — covering any messaging that "promotes or opposes a particular social, political, or public policy position" — could restrict researchers from communicating with APFED about findings that carry health policy implications for eosinophil disease treatment. • The prohibition on conference attendance costs (§200.432) impedes researchers from presenting findings in a timely manner. • The prohibition on publication costs (§200.461) impedes timely publication of federally funded research and dissemination of findings. Taken together, rare disease patients would bear disproportionate consequences of the proposed rule. APFED strongly urges OMB to withdraw this proposed rule. Federal research has helped rare disease patients because it has been structured around scientific merit, independent peer review, and long-term research commitments insulated from short-term political pressures. This rule dismantles each of those features without legislative authorization.

Conclusion While I support government efficiency, the proposed rules introduce arbitrary political control and an exhausting layer of administrative red tape. Ultimately, these barriers will hit rural communities, small historical organizations, and independent, disabled creators the hardest. I strongly urge OMB to withdraw these provisions. Sincerely, Cecelia Otto - Independent Musician, Music Historian, and Founder of American Songline (www.americansongline.com)

National Association for Bilingual Education (NABE) Statement on Proposed OMB Uniform Guidance Revisions To: Office of Management and Budget (OMB) Cc: Taylor & Francis Group; STM Association Subject: NABE Statement in Opposition to Proposed Restrictions on Publication and Subscription Costs under Uniform Guidance (2 CFR Part 200) The National Association for Bilingual Education (National Association for Bilingual Education) respectfully submits this statement in response to the proposed revisions to the Uniform Guidance (2 CFR Part 200), specifically Sections 200.454 and 200.461. NABE is a national professional membership organization dedicated to advancing bilingual education, multilingualism, and equitable educational opportunities for English learners across the United States. Our work includes disseminating research, convening national conferences, publishing practitioner-oriented resources, and supporting educators and researchers in the field of bilingual and dual language education. While NABE is impacted by federal policy environments that shape scholarly communication, it is important to note that NABE has not utilized NPD (National Professional Development) federal funds for publication costs or journal subscription expenses. Our publishing and dissemination activities are supported through non-federal and organizationally generated resources. We are concerned that the proposed revisions, specifically §200.454 (making journal subscriptions unallowable) and §200.461 (making publication costs, including article processing charges and open access fees, unallowable), would significantly restrict the ability of scholars, educators, and professional associations to disseminate research and practice-based knowledge. Even where federal funds are not directly used for publishing expenses, these restrictions would create broader systemic barriers to scholarly communication, reduce access to peer-reviewed research, and limit knowledge dissemination in fields that rely on interdisciplinary collaboration and practitioner access. If implemented, these provisions would have a chilling effect on the dissemination of research in bilingual and multilingual education, a field that depends heavily on accessible publication channels to bridge research and classroom practice. Reduced access to journals and limitations on publication pathways would disproportionately affect educators, graduate students, and smaller professional associations who rely on published scholarship to inform instruction, policy, and program development. Ultimately, this would undermine efforts to improve educational equity for multilingual learners. NABE respectfully urges the Office of Management and Budget to withdraw these proposed restrictions and retain the current allowability of reasonable publication and subscription costs under federal awards, ensuring continued access to scholarly communication and safeguarding the integrity and dissemination of research. Thank you for the opportunity to provide comments on this important matter. Sincerely, National Association for Bilingual Education (NABE) Washington, DC, USA

These intermediaries are often the reason federal programs reach eligible farmers. Fixed amount awards, simplified subawards, and advance payment options can make it possible for smaller organizations and farmer-led groups to participate. We respectfully request that OMB withdraw or substantially revise the proposed rule; remove or narrow the proposed discretionary termination and suspension provisions; clarify which USDA and agriculture-related programs would be affected; protect reliance interests; preserve advance payment options, fixed amount awards, and simplified subawards; ensure financial and programmatic support can reach all interested farmers, and analyze the impact on farmers and farmer-serving organizations. We urge OMB not to finalize the proposed rule as written.

Scientific research advances knowledge and informs public policy only when it is evaluated through rigorous, independent peer review. This process helps ensure that funding decisions are based on scientific merit, methodological rigor, feasibility, and potential impact rather than personal preferences, political considerations, or ideological agendas. The strength of peer review lies in its reliance on subject-matter expertise. Political appointees play an important role in setting broad policy priorities and ensuring accountability for public funds. History demonstrates that many transformative discoveries—from fundamental advances in medicine and public health to breakthroughs in technology and environmental science—emerged from research selected through expert scientific evaluation rather than political preference. Preserving an independent peer-review process helps ensure that public research investments are directed toward the highest-quality science and that funding decisions remain grounded in evidence, expertise, and the pursuit of knowledge. Philanthropy New York Opposes Proposed OMB Uniform Guidance Revisions Comment submitted July 2026 | Docket OMB-2026-0034* Philanthropy New York is submitting formal comments calling on the Office of Management and Budget to withdraw its proposed revisions to the Uniform Guidance governing federal financial assistance. If implemented, the proposal would make permanent what the New York philanthropic and nonprofit sector has spent the past year and a half struggling to survive: an unpredictable, politically driven federal grantmaking environment that disrupts essential services, burdens responsible funders, and undermines the transparent, community-centered partnership that federal programs were designed to support. We urge OMB to start over. Who we are Philanthropy New York (PNY) is a membership association of nearly 300 foundations and corporate giving programs across the New York region. For more than four decades, we have worked to promote effective, strategic philanthropy and to strengthen the nonprofit organizations our members fund — including the many grantees who rely on federal awards to deliver essential services in housing, health, education, legal services, the arts, and community development. Our public policy work rests on a core belief that laws and policies are strongest when they promote a just and equitable society, stand in allyship with the nonprofit sector, and support a more collaborative, transparent relationship between government and the communities it serves. A sector already under strain We do not raise these concerns abstractly. Over the past year and a half, our members and their grantees have navigated abrupt funding cancellations, frozen and restored awards, and surging community need — all at the same time. Foundations have stepped in with bridge funding and emergency technical assistance, but private philanthropy cannot and should not substitute for stable, predictable federal partnership. This proposal would codify that instability as permanent policy rather than address it. Our principal concerns Unchecked termination authority. The proposal would allow agencies to end a federal award whenever it no longer aligns with "Federal agency priorities or the national interest as they exist at the time of the termination" — a standard that shifts with each change in administration. Grantees would have no enforceable right to challenge such a determination. A foundation cannot responsibly commit multi-year co-investment alongside a federal award that can be withdrawn without cause or appeal. Political pre-screening. The rule would require senior political appointees to approve notices of award and allow them to exclude proposals that do not align with "agency priorities and the national interest" — without any obligation to explain the rejection. This replaces objective merit review with a process that can exclude grantees based on viewpoint or political disfavor, rather than community need or congressional intent. *A vague and overbroad DEI restriction.* The proposal bars federal funding from supporting "unlawful" diversity, equity, and inclusion activity without defining the term with precision. Many DEI-related practices — including programs serving immigrant families and communities that have experienced longstanding federal disinvestment — have been upheld by courts as fully permissible. A definition this vague will chill lawful, court-tested work across the sector. New burdens on pass-through entities. Several PNY members re-grant federal dollars to nonprofit subrecipients. The proposal would require those foundations to ensure their own grantees do not "significantly damage the reputation" of the funding agency or the federal government — and work toward termination of the subaward if they determine such damage is occurring. This asks funders to police their grantees' conduct and speech against an undefined federal standard, which is inconsistent with the independent judgment that responsible grantmaking requires. Restrictions reaching nonpartisan civic engagement. Proposed limits on the use of award funds for voter-related activity are written broadly enough to create confusion for nonprofits engaged in well-established, nonpartisan civic participation work — including regional funder collaboratives supporting get-out-the-vote efforts reaching hundreds of thousands of New Yorkers. What we're asking for PNY urges OMB to withdraw the proposal. If it proceeds, we call on OMB to restore meaningful appeal rights for grantees facing termination; define vague terms narrowly and consistent with existing case law; remove the pass-through policing requirement; narrow the civic engagement provision; and return to the transparent, criteria-based process that has historically allowed community need — not political preference — to determine who receives federal support.
