What we see now

The docket has one clock. Comments pile into the evening and all but stop from about 1am to mid-afternoon, and that shape has held the entire month even as daily volume swung from dozens to thousands. A genuinely organic public commenting on its own time would smear across the working day; this doesn't. The steadiness is the tell, whatever governs the timing stayed constant while the volume moved.

What we expect toward July 13

Deadlines pull comments forward, so daily counts should climb into the final stretch. Watch whether the evening-only shape survives that climb. If it does, the platform's posting cadence is driving the clock, not people. If the daytime hours start filling in, that points to real individuals submitting late, which usually means the organic opposition is mobilizing.

One caveat. These are posting and modify times the API returns, not verified submission times, so the hour view shows when the record appeared, not necessarily when a person hit send.

What we see now

Two different things share this chart. The teal is opposition, and it arrives the way real public comment does, unevenly, comment by comment. The gold is the single pro-rule template, and it doesn't trickle, it pulses. It came in a first wave of 1,930 copies cresting at 755 on 06-15, went quiet, then returned for a second push of 582 in late June. Clean bursts against a steady baseline are the signature of an organized drop, not a grassroots swell. Support now stands at 2,598, and 97% of it is that one letter.

What we expect toward July 13

Rulemaking dockets almost always spike in their final days, so expect the tallest bars of the window in the run-up to July 13. Given how the template has behaved, plan on at least one more gold wave before the close; campaigns like this usually save a push for the deadline. Expect opposition to surge too, since scientific societies mobilize their members late. A big pro wave could nudge the support share up from today's 5.6%, but opposition's individual base is deep enough to stay the clear majority. The cumulative line flattened once the mid-June rush banked most of the volume; a deadline surge would bend it up one more time.

What we see now

Three in four comments carry a name. Unremarkable on its own, but it fits the pattern the rest of the sheet keeps drawing: the opposition signs and writes for itself, while the copied template supplies most of the thin support. The named majority is doing the individual work; the anonymous quarter mixes genuine privacy with bulk filings.

What we expect toward July 13

Watch the anonymous share as the deadline nears. If another templated wave lands, the anonymous and repeated-name slices tend to swell with it, since bulk campaigns lean on blank or recycled names. A rising named share instead would signal real people filing late. The mix will tell you which side is driving the close.

One caveat. A name here is self-reported and unverified. It can be blank, a pseudonym, or reused, so this is a texture measure, not an identity check.

What we see now

One bar towers over the rest. The pro-rule template at 2,508 copies is several times larger than the biggest opposition letter, and it has grown faster than anything else in the docket, from a couple hundred copies a few weeks ago to where it sits now. That's the asymmetry in one picture: support is one letter reproduced thousands of times, while opposition, even where it shares language, breaks into many smaller letters and a long tail of one-off comments that never cluster at all.

What we expect toward July 13

Expect the pro cluster to jump again with any deadline wave, and don't be surprised if new opposition templates appear too; organized groups often circulate their own form letters in the final week, showing up as fresh red bars. The number to watch is how much of the total still sits outside every cluster, as long as that individual tail stays large, the opposition's authenticity advantage holds.

One caveat. Clustering on a shared opening catches copy-paste letters but misses reworded ones, so these counts are a floor on templating, not a ceiling.

What we see now

When commenters name a section, they name the ones that move power. §200.205 (merit review shifting toward senior appointees) and §200.340 (termination for agency priorities) lead by a wide margin, ahead of the cost provisions on publications and conferences. In their own words, people are most alarmed that the rule changes who decides and who can pull an award, not the line-item economics.

What we expect toward July 13

The most detailed comments tend to arrive last, and detailed comments are the ones that cite sections, so expect these counts to climb and the governance provisions to stay on top. If a late template circulates that names sections, whichever it cites will jump suddenly, a one- or two-day spike in a single provision is itself a sign of a coordinated letter rather than independent objection.

One caveat. This counts only explicit section numbers, so it undercounts; many comments oppose these ideas in plain language without citing a number.

What we see now

The two groups lean opposite ways. Among names the tool can classify, the pro-rule form-letter signers skew male, 57/43 male to female, while everyone else skews female, 43/57, about a fifteen-point swing between the copied-support crowd and the rest of the docket. The pro signers are also a little easier to classify (5% unrecognized names versus 10% for everyone else), meaning their names skew toward common, easily-matched forms. Neither gap is huge, but both point the same way, and both are consistent with the pro side being a curated list rather than a cross-section of the public.

What we expect toward July 13

If another pro wave lands, expect the same male-leaning, high-classifiability fingerprint, that sameness is part of what makes a wave look templated. A late opposition surge should look like the docket already does, closer to even, with a larger share of names the tool simply can't place.

How this is done, and where it breaks We take each signer's first name and look it up in an offline name-to-gender dictionary, mapping male and mostly-male to male, female and mostly-female to female, and everything else, unisex names, initials, and names the dictionary doesn't know, to unclassified. This is a coarse, biased proxy, read it only for large directional gaps, never small ones. The dictionary is Western-name-heavy, so many non-Western names land in unclassified and are undercounted. A first name is not gender: the method cannot see non-binary or trans identities and will misgender people. Names are self-reported and unverified, and anonymous filers (about 24% of the docket) are excluded because they have no name to read. Treat this page as suggestive texture that happens to agree with the other figures, not as a measured demographic fact.
